What is Toronto's Skate Culture Like?

Skate culture in the heart of Toronto is vibrant, dynamic, and deeply embedded in the city's urban landscape. From the iconic Dundas Square to the gritty streets of Queen West, Toronto's skate scene thrives on creativity, freedom, and self-expression. The city's diverse, ever-changing backdrop provides endless inspiration for skaters, blending street art, music, and fashion into a unique urban vibe. Local skateparks like the ever-popular Bellwoods and Exhibition Place serve as hubs for skaters of all levels, while the culture itself continues to influence Toronto's youth, offering a sense of community and rebellion against the conventional. This is my ode to skateboarding, a reflection of my experiences, my favourite spots like Graffiti Alley and the Art Gallery of Ontario, and my takes on the passionate, fearless people who bring this culture to life every day.

A Brief History on Skating in Toronto

Toronto skateboarding has a long history that mirrors the general evolution of the sport in North America. Skateboarding in Toronto began in the early 1970s as a byproduct of surfing culture. Toronto skateboarders first skated using homemade decks, typically using roller skate wheels attached to wooden boards. The first big boom for skateboarding was in the mid-70s when pro equipment came into production, and the first skateboard parks. One of the most well-known of these at the time was "The Edge" in the city. By the 1980s, Toronto skateboarding gained more popularity, especially because it started becoming more focused on street skating. Skaters started using urban terrain like stairs, rails, and ledges as skate spots. This change in skate culture created makeshift skate spots around the city, including the iconic Dundas Square and financial district. In the 1990s and 2000s, the skate culture in Toronto diversified with numerous local shops, skate teams, and events supporting the community. The skateparks in the city grew, and Toronto began hosting international competitions, the biggest of which being the X Games. Today, Toronto has a thriving skateboarding culture, with an active community that continues to innovate the sport.

Toronto Skate Pioneers

Toronto has produced some of the most talented and influential skateboarders in the scene. These skaters have not only mastered their craft but have also helped shape the city's skate culture, inspiring new generations. From local legends to international pros, their impact on the sport is undeniable.

Andy Anderson is a very creative and talented Canadian skateboarder. He was born in White Rock, British Columbia. Andy became world-renowned for fusing the new park and street styles with traditional freestyle skating. What makes him stand out from the majority of skaters is his capability to transition smoothly from hard tricks, handplants, and board flips. Anderson became famous after competing in the 2020 Tokyo Olympics, where skateboarding was featured for the first time in the Olympics. His signature helmet, which he is never seen without, is now a part of his identity. He differs from most professional skaters in that he values creativity over traditional style and always tries to do something different on a skateboard. He has also created a signature deck with Powell-Peralta that is designed to serve both street and freestyle skating. With his positive attitude, huge respect for skate heritage, and creative approach, Andy Anderson continues to inspire skaters worldwide.

Fay De Fazio Ebert is becoming increasingly popular in Canadian skateboarding because she is fearless and possesses a smooth style. She is from Toronto and has quickly become well known in competitive skateboarding, especially among female street skaters. Fay, despite her young age, has already accomplished great things, showcasing her talent in national as well as global competitions. Her biggest achievement is that she competed in the 2023 X Games and other top skate competitions, where she impressed individuals with her smooth style and proficient tricks. She has also been recognized for effortless maneuvering on rails, stairs, and ledges, showing she can keep up with experienced professionals. Fay De Fazio Ebert is part of the new wave of Canadian skaters. She continues to break barriers and encourages young skaters, especially women, to take up skateboarding. With her determination and skills, she is bound to be one of Toronto's most influential skaters.

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fay_De_Fazio_Ebert

Morgan Smith is one of Toronto's best and technically soundest skateboarders. He is known for his incredibly smooth style and precise execution of tricks and has dominated the world of street skating for a long time. His ability to effortlessly land complex flip-in and flip-out tricks on ledges, rails, and manual pads has cemented his reputation as a top-level technician. One of the pinnacle moments in his career was triumphing at Tampa Am in 2009, a competition revered as one of the most prestigious amateur skateboarding events globally. Since that remarkable victory, he has appeared in numerous skate videos and collaborated with renowned brands such as Blind Skateboards, Etnies, Red Bull, and Venture Trucks. Morgan's skating style incorporates creativity, control, and consistency and has been a huge influence on the younger generation of skaters. Whether he's releasing a new video part or competing, he's still proving why he's one of Toronto's most successful and respected skateboarders.
